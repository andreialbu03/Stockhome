About

Note, all information presented below comes from source [3]

Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. (AMD) is an American multinational semiconductor company based in Santa Clara, California, that develops computer processors and related technologies for business and consumer markets. While it initially manufactured its own processors, the company later outsourced its manufacturing, a practice known as going fabless, after GlobalFoundries was spun off in 2009. AMD's main products include microprocessors, motherboard chipsets, embedded processors and graphics processors for servers, workstations, personal computers and embedded system applications.

History

Advanced Micro Devices was formally incorporated by Jerry Sanders, along with seven of his colleagues from Fairchild Semiconductor, on May 1, 1969. Sanders, an electrical engineer who was the director of marketing at Fairchild, had, like many Fairchild executives, grown frustrated with the increasing lack of support, opportunity, and flexibility within the company. He later decided to leave to start his own semiconductor company. Robert Noyce, who had developed the first silicon integrated circuit at Fairchild in 1959, had left Fairchild together with Gordon Moore and founded the semiconductor company Intel in July 1968. In 1972, they took AMD public and began making computer chips. In 1991, AMD introduced its first microprocessor, the Am386. In 1996, AMD launched its first x86 microprocessor designed entirely by them, called the K5. Additionally, 1996 was the year that they acquired NexGen, which cost them $857 million.

AMD has come a long way from where it began. Currently, its biggest competitor is Intel — another semiconductor company — however recently AMD has shown more compelling improvements in their products.

Products

AMD specializes in manufacturing microprocessors, specifically central processing units or CPUs. They provide CPUs for both personal, and enterprise use. AMD also produces graphics products and graphics processing units or GPUs.

An interesting fact is that both the Xbox and Playstation consoles use AMD’s custom processors.
